Types of Stocks

There are many types of stocks ranging in a whole multitude of sectors. Below is a list of the different sectors and stock types you can invest in depending on your interests and what you believe is going to do well.

Stock Sectors

Stock Types

1) Technology

Companies that make software, hardware, semiconductors, or tech services (e.g., Apple, Microsoft).

2) Healthcare

Firms involved in medicine, medical devices, biotech, or healthcare services (e.g., Pfizer, Johnson & Johnson).

3) Financials

Banks, insurance companies, and other financial services (e.g., JPMorgan Chase, Goldman Sachs).

4) Consumer Discretionary

Businesses selling non-essential goods and services like cars, clothing, and entertainment (e.g., Nike, Disney).

5) Consumer Staples

Companies producing essential products like food, beverages, and household goods (e.g., Procter & Gamble, Coca-Cola).

6) Energy

Oil, gas, renewable energy, and related services (e.g., ExxonMobil, Chevron).

7) Industrials

Firms that make machinery, tools, or provide industrial services (e.g., Boeing, Caterpillar).

8) Utilities

Companies providing electricity, gas, and water services (e.g., Duke Energy, National Grid).

9) Materials

Producers of raw materials like metals, chemicals, and construction materials (e.g., Dow, Rio Tinto).

10) Communication Services

Media, telecom, and internet services companies (e.g., Verizon, Netflix).

11) Real Estate

Companies that develop, manage, or invest in property (e.g., American Tower, Simon Property Group).

12) Consumer & Tech Combined

(sometimes called "Information Technology" broadly) – A broader category including software, IT services, and electronics.

1) Common Stocks

Regular shares that give owners voting rights and potential dividends. Prices can go up or down depending on the company's performance.

2) Preferred Stocks

Shares that usually don't have voting rights but pay fixed dividends and have priority over common stock if the company goes bankrupt.

3) Growth Stocks

Companies expected to grow faster than the market average. They often reinvest profits instead of paying big dividends, so returns come mainly from price increases.

4) Value Stocks

Stocks priced lower than their perceived worth, often because the company is temporarily underperforming. Investors buy them hoping the price will rise to reflect true value.

5) Dividend Stocks

Companies that regularly pay part of their profits to shareholders. Good for generating steady income.

6) Blue-Chip Stocks

Large, well-established companies with a strong track record of reliability, stability, and steady dividends (e.g., Coca-Cola, Johnson & Johnson).

7) Small-Cap Stocks

Companies with a smaller market value, often under $2 billion. Higher risk but can offer big growth potential.

8) Mid-Cap Stocks

Medium-sized companies, usually between $2–10 billion in market value. Balanced risk and growth potential.

9) Large-Cap Stocks

Big companies, usually over $10 billion in market value. Generally stable with moderate growth.

10) Penny Stocks

Very low-priced stocks (often under $5) from small or new companies. High risk but can sometimes offer huge gains.

11) International/Global Stocks

Shares of companies based outside your home country, offering diversification and exposure to global growth.

Buying and Selling Stocks

Once you understand the different sectors and stocks you can invest in, the next step is buying and selling stocks. So how do you do this?

Buying:

The easiest way to do this is through a brokerage account, which is like a bridge between you and the stock market. You deposit money into the account, then use it to purchase shares of companies you want to invest in. When you buy a stock, you're paying the current market price per share, and then you own a piece of that company.

Selling:

Selling works the same way in reverse, you tell your broker to sell your shares at either the current market price or at a set price you choose. The money from the sale goes back into your brokerage account, and you can either reinvest it or withdraw it. With online apps and websites, you can now buy and sell faster and more easily than ever before.

Understanding Market, Limit, and Stop Orders

Below is more detailed information about the types of buys and sells you can make when trading stocks. For convenience sake, it is typically easier to create a market order.

  1. Market Order – Buy or sell a stock immediately at the best available current price. Fast but the price can fluctuate slightly.
  2. Limit Order – Set a specific price you're willing to pay (buy) or accept (sell). The trade only happens if the stock reaches that price.
  3. Stop Order (Stop-Loss Order) – Automatically sells a stock when it hits a certain price, helping limit losses if the stock drops.
  4. Stop-Limit Order – Combines a stop order and a limit order: once the stop price is reached, it becomes a limit order to sell or buy at a specific price.
  5. Trailing Stop Order – Sets a sell price at a fixed percentage or amount below the stock's highest price since you bought it, allowing you to lock in profits while limiting losses.
  6. All-or-None (AON) Order – Ensures that your entire order is executed at once; it won't partially fill.
  7. Good 'Til Canceled (GTC) Order – Remains active until it's executed or you cancel it (unlike a standard order that expires at the end of the trading day).
  8. Day Order – Only valid for the trading day; if it's not executed by the market close, it's canceled automatically.
